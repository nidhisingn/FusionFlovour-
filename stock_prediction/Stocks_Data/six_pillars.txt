======================================================================
                 6 PILLARS OF QUALITATIVE ANALYSIS
======================================================================

Goal:
  Use this framework to judge *business quality* (not just numbers).


----------------------------------------------------------------------
PILLAR 1 — BUSINESS MODEL
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Core Question:
  How does the company make money and is it sustainable?

- Understand the revenue mechanism — product, service, subscription, transaction
- Recurring revenue is better than one-time revenue
- Does the model get stronger as it scales?
- Can it be easily copied by competitors?

Key check:
  If the company disappeared tomorrow, would customers genuinely miss it,
  or would they easily find an alternative?

---

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PILLAR 2 — COMPETITIVE MOAT
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Core Question:
  What stops competitors from taking market share?t

Moat Types (examples):
  - Brand          : HUL, Nestle, Asian Paints
  - Switching cost : TCS, Infosys (replacing IT systems is costly)
  - Cost advantage : Divi's Labs (low-cost API manufacturer)
  - Network effect : Zerodha, exchanges
  - Regulatory     : Coal India, airport operators

Key check:
  If a well-funded competitor entered tomorrow, how long would it take
  to seriously damage this company?

---

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PILLAR 3 — MANAGEMENT QUALITY
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Core Question:
  Are the people running this company trustworthy and capable?

- Did past guidance match actual results?
- What do they do with profits? (reinvest wisely vs random acquisitions)
- Promoter holding high and not pledged?
- Do they communicate failures openly?
- Does management own significant shares personally?

Red flags (watchlist):
  -> Frequent auditor changes
  -> Related-party transactions
  -> Qualified audit reports
  -> Sudden CFO/director resignations
  -> Promoter pledging increasing every quarter

---

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PILLAR 4 — INDUSTRY TAILWINDS
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Core Question:
  Is the overall industry growing and will it continue?

Examples:
  - IT      : Global digital transformation
  - Pharma  : Aging population, India as global manufacturing hub
  - FMCG    : Premiumization, rising middle class
  - Finance : Financialization of Indian savings
  - Infra   : Government capex cycle

Key check:
  Is this industry growing faster than GDP?
  Is it early-stage, growth-stage, or mature-stage?

---

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PILLAR 5 — COMPANY-SPECIFIC RISKS
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Core Question:
  What could go wrong and how bad would it be?

Risk Types (examples):
  - Concentration : One product/drug = 30% revenue (FDA risk in pharma)
  - Regulatory    : Paytm — RBI restrictions overnight
  - Disruption    : EVs disrupting OEMs, fintech disrupting banks
  - Promoter      : Integrity / governance issues
  - Global         : Export dependence (US recession risk for IT)

Key check:
  If this risk materializes, is it a temporary drawdown
  or does it permanently damage the business?

---

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PILLAR 6 — COMPETITIVE POSITIONING
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Core Question:
  Is this company gaining or losing within its sector?

- Track revenue market share over 5 years
- Gaining share = compounder
- Losing share = value trap (looks cheap but keeps getting cheaper)
- Read concall transcripts for management tone

Positive vs warning language (concall signals):

POSITIVE
  - "Deal wins accelerating"
  - "Pipeline at record levels"
  - "Margin expansion expected"
  - "Gaining market share"

WARNING
  - "Macro headwinds"
  - "Softness in demand"
  - "Recovery expected in H2"
  - "Clients deferring decisions"

---

----------------------------------------------------------------------
QUICK REVISION CARD
----------------------------------------------------------------------

P1 — Business Model
  -> How do they earn? Is it recurring? Can it be copied?

P2 — Moat
  -> Brand / Switching cost / Cost advantage / Network / Regulatory

P3 — Management
  -> Trustworthy? Track record? Promoter holding? Capital allocation?

P4 — Tailwinds
  -> Industry growth? Macro trends supportive for 5–10 years?

P5 — Risks
  -> Concentration? Regulatory? Disruption? Promoter? Global?

P6 — Competitive Position
  -> Gaining or losing market share within sector?

----------------------------------------------------------------------
WHERE DOES THIS COME FROM?
----------------------------------------------------------------------

These 6 pillars are not from one single source. They are a distillation of frameworks from the world's most respected investors and books:

**Warren Buffett** — Pillar 2 (Moat concept) and Pillar 3 (Management quality). He has written about economic moats and management integrity extensively in his **Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters** — all freely available at berkshirehathaway.com. Some of the best investing education written in plain English.

**Peter Lynch** — Pillar 1 (Business Model understanding). His book **"One Up on Wall Street"** says invest in businesses you understand from daily life. He turned qualitative observations into massive returns managing Fidelity Magellan Fund.

**Phil Fisher** — Pillar 3 and 6 (Management quality and competitive position). His book **"Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits"** introduced the concept of **"Scuttlebutt"** — talking to customers, suppliers, competitors to understand a business qualitatively before investing.

**Michael Porter** — Pillar 2 and 4 (Moat and Industry analysis). His **"Five Forces Framework"** from Harvard Business School is the foundation of competitive analysis taught in every MBA program worldwide.

**Pat Dorsey** — Most directly, Pillar 2 is almost entirely from his book **"The Little Book That Builds Wealth"** where he systematically categorizes moat types exactly as explained above. Pat Dorsey was Director of Equity Research at Morningstar.

**Sanjay Bakshi** — Indian context for all 6 pillars. Professor at MDI Gurgaon and fund manager. His lectures and writings on qualitative investing in Indian markets are freely available and are some of the best India-specific investing content available.

RECOMMENDED READING (in order)
----------------------------------------------------------------------

BOOK 1 -> One Up on Wall Street — Peter Lynch
          (easiest + practical)

BOOK 2 -> The Little Book That Builds Wealth — Pat Dorsey
          (best on moats)

BOOK 3 -> Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits — Phil Fisher
          (management quality + qualitative research)

BOOK 4 -> Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters — Warren Buffett
          (free online; start with 1977–2000)

BOOK 5 -> The Intelligent Investor — Benjamin Graham
          (valuation discipline, margin of safety)

These five sources cover ~90% of what a serious long-term investor needs.
Everything else is refinement on top of these fundamentals.
